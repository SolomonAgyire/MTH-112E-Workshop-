Field Details

Study Strategy to be Used Application of Rules (Quick recall and verification)
Instructions Analyze the three functions below to determine the type and equation of the required asymptote.

Problem A: Vertical Asymptote (VA)

Instruction: Find the Vertical Asymptote(s) for \(f(x) = \frac{x-4}{x^2-4x+3}\).

Step-by-step Solution

Step 1: Factor the denominator: \(x^2 - 4x + 3 = (x - 3)(x - 1)\).
Note: VA occurs where the simplified denominator is zero. Check for common factors (holes).
Step 2: Set factors to zero: \(x - 3 = 0 \rightarrow x = 3\); \(x - 1 = 0 \rightarrow x = 1\).

Neither zero cancels with the numerator \((x-4)\).

Solution: The VAs are the lines \(x = 3\) and \(x = 1\).

Problem B: Horizontal Asymptote (HA)

Instruction: Find the Horizontal Asymptote for \(g(x) = \frac{9x^3-2x^2+1}{3x^3+7}\).

Step-by-step Solution

Step 1: Compare Degrees (n vs. d): Degree of numerator (n) is 3. Degree of denominator (d) is 3. Since n = d, use the ratio of leading coefficients.
Rule: When degrees are equal, HA is \(y = \frac{\text{leading coefficient of N}}{\text{leading coefficient of D}}\).
Step 2: Use Leading Coefficients: Numerator leading coefficient is 9. Denominator leading coefficient is 3.

Ratio is \(\frac{9}{3}\).

Solution: The HA is the line \(y = 3\).

Problem C: Slant Asymptote (SA)

Instruction: Find the Slant Asymptote for \(h(x) = \frac{x^2-3x+1}{x+2}\).

Step-by-step Solution

Step 1: Compare Degrees (n vs. d): n = 2, d = 1. Since n = d + 1, a SA exists.
Note: The divisor must be linear \((x + 2)\) for synthetic division to apply.
Step 2: Perform Synthetic Division: Use c = -2 on the coefficients 1, -3, 1.

Remember to ignore the remainder.

Synthetic Division Steps:

-2 |  1  -3   1
   |    -2  10
   |  1  -5  11
Step 3: Identify Quotient: The quotient is \(1x - 5\).

The remainder (11) is ignored for the SA equation.

Solution: The SA is the line \(y = x - 5\).

üí™ Workout Part 1: Function Fundamentals and Domain (15 mins)

Field Details
Content to Cover Relation vs. Function (VLT), Function Evaluation, Domain (Rational & Radical)
Collaborative Learning Technique Small Group Problem-Solving (Groups of 3)
Instructions Students work in groups to solve the following problems.

Q1 (Function Check): Does the relation define y as a function? \(\{(5, 8), (-1, 4), (5, -3)\}\)

Check: Does any input (x) map to more than one output (y)?

Step 1: List the domain (x-values): 5, -1, 5.
Note: The input x=5 appears twice.
Step 2: Check mappings: 5 ‚Üí 8 AND 5 ‚Üí -3.

One input (5) has two different outputs (8 and -3).

Solution: No, it is not a function.

Q2 (Evaluation): Evaluate \(f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 1\) for \(f(2a)\)

Goal: Practice substitution and simplification.

Step 1: Replace x with (2a): \(f(2a) = (2a)^2 - 4(2a) + 1\).
Step 2: Simplify the terms: \((2a)^2 = 4a^2\); \(-4(2a) = -8a\).
Solution: \(f(2a) = 4a^2 - 8a + 1\).

Q3 (Rational Domain): Find the domain of \(f(x) = \frac{x+7}{x^2-16}\)

Rule: Denominator cannot be zero.

Step 1: Set the denominator not equal to zero: \(x^2 - 16 \neq 0\).
Step 2: Factor and solve: \((x - 4)(x + 4) \neq 0\).

\(x \neq 4\) and \(x \neq -4\).

Solution: Domain: \(\{x | x \neq 4, x \neq -4\}\) or \((-\infty, -4) \cup (-4, 4) \cup (4, \infty)\).

Q4 (Radical Domain): Find the domain of \(g(x) = \sqrt{3x+9}\)

Rule: Radicand (stuff under the even root) must be greater than or equal to zero.

Step 1: Set the radicand ‚â• 0: \(3x + 9 \geq 0\).
Step 2: Solve for x: \(3x \geq -9 \rightarrow x \geq -3\).
Solution: Domain: \([-3, \infty)\).

üí™ Workout Part 2: Polynomial Zeros and Factorization (15 mins)

Field Details
Content to Cover Factorization, Synthetic Division, Remainder/Factor Theorems, Rational Zero Theorem
Collaborative Learning Technique Small Group Problem-Solving (Groups of 3)
Instructions Students work in groups to solve the following problems.

Q1 (Factor by Grouping): Factor completely: \(x^3 + 5x^2 - 4x - 20\)

Goal: Review non-synthetic factoring methods.

Step 1: Group the first two terms and the last two terms: \((x^3 + 5x^2) + (-4x - 20)\).
Step 2: Factor out the Greatest Common Factor (GCF) from each group: \(x^2(x + 5) - 4(x + 5)\).
Note: Note the sign change in the second group.
Step 3: Factor out the common binomial factor \((x + 5)\): \((x + 5)(x^2 - 4)\).
Step 4: Factor the Difference of Squares: \((x^2 - 4) = (x - 2)(x + 2)\).
Solution: \((x + 5)(x - 2)(x + 2)\).

Q2 (Synthetic Division): Find the quotient and remainder: \(\frac{4x^4+2x^2-5x+1}{x-1}\)

Note: Must use a placeholder for the missing \(x^3\) term.
Step 1: Use c = 1 (from x - 1) and coefficients 4, 0, 2, -5, 1.
Step 2: Perform synthetic division. 
 1 |  4   0   2  -5   1
   |     4   4   6   1
   |  4   4   6   1   2
Step 3: Identify the quotient polynomial (degree one less than the dividend).
Solution: \(Q(x) = 4x^3 + 4x^2 + 6x + 1\), \(R = 2\).

Q3 (Factor Theorem): Use synthetic division to determine if \((x + 3)\) is a factor of \(f(x) = x^3 + 8x^2 + 15x\)

Rule: If the remainder R = 0, then \((x + 3)\) is a factor.

Step 1: Use c = -3 and coefficients 1, 8, 15, 0 (must use 0 for the constant term).
Step 2: Perform division. 
-3 |  1   8  15   0
   |    -3 -15   0
   |  1   5   0   0
Step 3: Check the remainder.

Since the remainder is 0, it is a factor.

Solution: Yes, \((x + 3)\) is a factor.

Q4 (Rational Zero Theorem): List all possible rational zeros for \(f(x) = 4x^3 - x^2 - 7x + 3\)

Rule: Possible zeros are \(\frac{P}{Q}\), where P is a factor of the constant (3) and Q is a factor of the leading coefficient (4).

Step 1: Identify factors of P (constant, 3) and Q (leading coefficient, 4).

P: ¬±1, ¬±3; Q: ¬±1, ¬±2, ¬±4.

Step 2: Create the \(\frac{P}{Q}\) list (include positive and negative versions).

\(\frac{P}{Q}\): \(\frac{1}{1}, \frac{3}{1}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}\).

Solution: The possible rational zeros are ¬±1, ¬±3, ¬±\(\frac{1}{2}\), ¬±\(\frac{3}{2}\), ¬±\(\frac{1}{4}\), ¬±\(\frac{3}{4}\).

üßä Cool-Down / Closing (10 mins)

Field Details
Content to Cover X and Y Intercepts, Parallel/Perpendicular Slopes, Zeros Synthesis
Collaborative Learning Technique Whole-class discussion
Instructions Solve the closing problems collaboratively, applying various concepts.

Q1 (Intercepts): Find the x- and y-intercepts of \(3x - 2y = 12\)

Goal: Find intercepts algebraically.

Step 1 (Y-Intercept): Set x = 0: \(3(0) - 2y = 12 \rightarrow -2y = 12\).

\(y = -6\).

Step 2 (X-Intercept): Set y = 0: \(3x - 2(0) = 12 \rightarrow 3x = 12\).

\(x = 4\).

Solution: Y-intercept: \((0, -6)\); X-intercept: \((4, 0)\).

Q2 (Parallel/Perpendicular Slopes): Find the slope of a line parallel to and a line perpendicular to \(2x + 5y = 10\)

Goal: Review slope definitions (m, -1/m).

Step 1: Convert to slope-intercept form \((y = mx + b)\) to find the original slope (m): \(5y = -2x + 10 \rightarrow y = \frac{-2}{5}x + 2\).

The original slope is \(m = \frac{-2}{5}\).

Step 2 (Parallel): A parallel line has the same slope.

Parallel slope = \(m = \frac{-2}{5}\).

Step 3 (Perpendicular): A perpendicular line has the negative reciprocal slope.

Perpendicular slope = \(-\frac{1}{m} = -\frac{1}{-2/5} = \frac{5}{2}\).

Solution: Parallel slope: \(\frac{-2}{5}\). Perpendicular slope: \(\frac{5}{2}\).

Q3 (Full Zeros Synthesis): Find all zeros of \(g(x) = x^3 + 2x^2 - 5x - 6\)

Step-by-step Solution

Step 1: List P/Q possibilities: P (factors of 6) = ¬±1, ¬±2, ¬±3, ¬±6. Q (factors of 1) = ¬±1. Possibilities: ¬±1, ¬±2, ¬±3, ¬±6.
Step 2: Test a value, c = 2, using synthetic division on 1, 2, -5, -6. 
 2 |  1   2  -5  -6
   |     2   8   6
   |  1   4   3   0
Step 3: Use the depressed polynomial \(x^2 + 4x + 3\) and factor it.

\((x + 3)(x + 1) = 0\).

Step 4: Solve the remaining factors for the last two zeros.

\(x = -3\) and \(x = -1\).

Solution: The zeros are \(x = 2\), \(x = -3\), \(x = -1\).
